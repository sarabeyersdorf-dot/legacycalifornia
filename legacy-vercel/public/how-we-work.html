A manifesto · plainly stated

How we
work.

Real estate is a transactional industry that pretends to be a relational one. We've tried to make it the other way around — relational on purpose, transactional only at the end, and never apologetic about taking longer than the brokerage chains.

Below: the one promise, ten tenets, the things we don't do, the process we do follow, and what it costs. In writing, so you can hold us to it.

"
The one promise

You will always work with
Sara or James. Never anyone else.

That is the entire promise. Every text, every email, every showing, every offer drafted, every inspection walked, every closing letter — comes from one of the two people whose names are on the door. We do not have a team. We do not have junior agents. We do not hand you off to a "transaction coordinator." If you reach us, you reach us. If you write a reply at 9 p.m. on a Tuesday, Sara reads it that night.

The math of this is uncomfortable on purpose. It caps how many clients we can take in a year, which caps how much we make, which caps how big we'll ever grow. We've decided that's the right trade. The boutique remains boutique because we keep choosing it to be.

— Sara & James · Signed, 2025 · Re-signed every January
§ I · Ten tenets

What we believe
about this work.

The ten things we keep going back to. The order matters; the first one is doing more work than the others.

I.

The right home is rarely the first home.

The home people end up loving is almost never the one they wrote in about. Good representation means widening the aperture before narrowing it — and being patient enough to do that without selling something prematurely.

II.

Volume is the enemy of care.

We do roughly forty closings a year, between us, on purpose. Any more and we'd be doing the math instead of doing the work. Any less and we couldn't sustainably run the brokerage. The number is calibrated, not aspirational.

III.

The hard thing should be said first.

Septic, well, snow access, fire zone, the neighbor's dog, the foundation crack, the seller's expectations. Anything we know that would matter to you, you hear in the first conversation, before you're emotionally committed. Surprises are bad form.

The fee is the fee, once.

Standard rate, paid at closing by one side only. No tiered service levels, no premium listings, no "we'll waive that for you" theater. The transparency on this is on purpose — see § Fees.

We don't chase you.

No drip campaigns. No "just checking in" emails on a 14-day cadence. If we follow up, we have something to say. If we don't, your inbox stays quiet. Subscribing to the Ledger is the closest thing we have to a marketing funnel, and it's a monthly newsletter you can unsubscribe from in one click.

§ II · Six nevers

Things we
do not do.

A profession this old accumulates a lot of conventions. These are the ones we've quietly retired.

Never · I

Dual agency.

If we represent the seller, we don't also represent the buyer. The conflict of interest is real, the savings are an illusion, and one side always feels short-changed by the end.

Never · II

Pocket listings without your consent.

Off-market deals happen — sometimes they're the right call. But your listing goes off-market only if you sign the line that says so, in writing, after we've explained the trade-off.

Never · III

Lead generation services.

We don't pay Zillow for your search history. We don't buy you as a lead from a national matching service. If you found us, you found us — and the introduction wasn't a transaction.

Never · IV

Cookie-cutter staging.

We won't rent the same gray sofa and the same brass-arc lamp that's in every flip in the county. Stage to the house, not the trend. Most listings we don't stage at all — they're already lovely.

Never · V

Pressure to "act fast."

If a property is the right one, it's right at the right pace. If we're saying "you'll lose it if you don't decide tonight," we're not advising — we're closing. We don't close.

Never · VI

Hand off to junior agents.

There are no junior agents. There is Sara, and there is James. The work either has our names on it or it isn't ours.

§ V · The honest questions

What people
actually ask.

Plain answers to the questions clients ask in the first or second meeting. If yours isn't here, send it.

Will Sara actually be at every showing?
Yes. Every one. If she physically can't be there for a scheduled tour — vacation, family emergency, the one week a year she's at the Calaveras Pie Auction — we reschedule. You will not be shown houses by anyone else. If that ever changes, we'll have stopped being us.
What if you're already representing someone else on a house I love?
We tell you, immediately. If we're representing the seller and you want to make an offer, we refer you to one of two agents we trust in the region — by name, with their direct line — so you get real representation. We never run dual agency.
How long does it usually take to find a home?
The honest median, looking at the last three years of our buyer-side work: four months from first call to keys. Some have been six weeks; one was nineteen months. We don't speed it up artificially. The right home is rarely the first home; it's also rarely the seventeenth.
What happens to my data and search history?
It lives in our system. It's yours. You can export it, delete it, or ask us to delete it on your behalf with one email. We never sell leads, never share with the brokerage chain (we aren't part of one), and the only ad-tracker on this site is one we use to know how many people read the Ledger. See our privacy policy for the full picture.
Do you work with first-time buyers?
Yes — when the math works. We're upfront: in the foothills, the median first-time-buyer entry point is around $450k, and we want to make sure the loan, the down payment, and the carrying costs are realistic before we start. If the budget's tight, we'll tell you what's possible and what isn't, in writing, before you fall in love with anything.
Can I work with you if I'm not in the Bay Area?
Most of our clients aren't — they're in Sacramento, Reno, LA, Seattle, Austin. We do plenty of "video tour first, in person later" representation. The trick is doing the video tours honestly — showing you the kitchen drainage and the back-fence line, not the curated Instagram angles.
What if I decide not to buy or sell anything?
That's an entirely fine outcome. We don't bill for hours or "consultation time." If the right thing for you is to wait, we'll tell you that, and we'll be here when the right thing changes. About one in five conversations we have ends with "not yet" — and they're often the ones that come back as long-term clients.
Are you ever going to sell the brokerage?
No. Asked and answered, in writing, since day one. We've turned down three acquisition offers from regional brokerages. Legacy is the two of us, and it will stay that way until we retire or step back voluntarily — at which point we'll either close it cleanly or hand it to someone whose values we know in our bones. We will not sell to a chain.
