Field notes · § I

The town that
launched a literature.

In the winter of 1865 an unknown young writer named Samuel Clemens sat down at the Angels Hotel bar and listened to a man named Ben Coon tell a yarn about a man, a frog, and a wager. Two weeks later the story was in a New York paper. Mark Twain was born.

The same hotel bar is still there, on the same block, on the same Main Street. The frog is still mythic. And every May, on the third weekend, the Calaveras County Fair & Jumping Frog Jubilee — running since 1928 — fills the fairgrounds with hundreds of frogs and the families that hand-raise them.

Angels Camp is also the operating address of Legacy Properties. Sara lives a mile from the fairgrounds. Most of her closings happen within a thirty-minute radius of her front porch.

Plate III · A short history of a tall tale

The frog that
made the writer.

A hundred and sixty years of one story, lived on six city blocks.

1865
A bar, a yarn, a notebook
Samuel Clemens, broke and unknown, hears Ben Coon tell a frog story at the Angels Hotel. He writes it down on the back of a letter.
1865
Published in New York
"The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County" runs in the New York Saturday Press. Reprinted everywhere within weeks. Twain is on the map.
1928
The Jubilee begins
Angels Camp celebrates the paving of Main Street with the first Jumping Frog Jubilee. It is never not held again — even in 1944.
1986
Brass plates on Main
The Hall of Fame Frogs project sets bronze frogs into the sidewalk, one per winning frog since 1928. Walk Main Street, count the names.
2025
Sara opens Legacy
After five years selling homes under other brokers, Sara opens Legacy Properties one block off Main — her own shingle at last.

A note from your agent · § VII
SC Sara Cooper · Owner-Broker · Lives here

A few words on Angels Camp —

I live here. My mailbox is a mile from the fairgrounds, and I owner-built our home inside the city limits. I've been in this town since 1999.

Angels Camp is the most-affordable town in the survey — by a margin that surprises Bay Area buyers when they first see the numbers. The trade-off is honest: it's a working town, not a tasting-room town. Murphys is twelve minutes north when you want that. Sonora is twenty-two minutes south when you need a hospital. Both are close enough to count as amenities.

If you're looking for the best dollar-per-square-foot in three counties, with a real Main Street, a real fair, and a real history — Angels Camp is the conversation. Come see me when you're in town — I'm usually around before nine.

— Sara
