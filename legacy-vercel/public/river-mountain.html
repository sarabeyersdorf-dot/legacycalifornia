Field notes · § I

Some buyers choose
a town. You're
choosing a creek.

There's a kind of buyer the Mother Lode quietly attracts. They don't want walkable Main Street. They want a quarter-mile of frontage on a year-round creek, a well that ran through the 2014 drought, and a road that ends at their gate.

This is who we cater to in Wilseyville, Mountain Ranch, West Point, Glencoe, Sheep Ranch, Mokelumne Hill, and across the county line into Placerville and Pollock Pines. None of these are towns the way Murphys is a town. They're constellations of parcels strung along the Mokelumne, the Cosumnes, the South Fork, the North Fork, Licking Fork, San Antonio Creek.

The transactions look different. The questions look different. The buyers look different. We've been writing this kind of contract for years — and James was buying water-rights opinions on rural parcels for a decade before that.

Who this is for —
01

The homesteader

Ten acres, a well, a barn, garden rows. Maybe chickens, maybe goats, maybe just a freezer full of venison and a smoker that gets used every Sunday.

02

The water buyer

Senior water right. A creek that runs in August. A pond that holds. The buyer who understands that California real estate is, fundamentally, water rights with a house on it.

03

The privacy seeker

No neighbors in sight. A gate at the road. The kind of buyer who reads the parcel map before they read the listing description.

04

The legacy parcel buyer

Forty acres or more. The kind of land you don't ever sell — you pass it. Often a buyer in their fifties who's been thinking about this since their thirties.

Plate IV · What we'll ask before any offer

Nine questions
before the listing photos.

01

Water right status

Senior, junior, riparian, appropriative, district. Which one you hold determines what happens in the next drought. Non-negotiable diligence.

02

Well yield & recovery

Gallons per minute, drawdown rate, last test date. Anything below 5 gpm with poor recovery is an irrigation-only well. We pull the records before we walk the property.

03

Septic age & type

Conventional, mound, sand-filter. Calaveras Environmental requires inspection on transfer. Age over 25 years = budget for replacement. $18–35K typical.

04

Fire zone designation

State Responsibility Area, Local Responsibility Area, Very-High Severity. Insurance reality varies wildly. Some parcels are non-insurable through standard carriers.

05

Road access & maintenance

Public, private, shared. If shared, is there a maintenance agreement recorded? Without one, you may inherit road grading disputes for the next decade.

06

Snow load & winter access

Above 3,500 ft, plan on snow. Above 4,500 ft, plan on chains. We pull historical NWS snowfall and ask the seller about Februaries.

07

Broadband & cell

Fiber is in most of West Point and Mountain Ranch. Starlink covers the rest. Cell varies by ridge. We test from the property before you write the offer.

08

Easements & setbacks

PG&E, neighbor access, mineral rights, BLM-adjacent. Title commitment review with you, line by line. Half the rural surprises live here.

09

What your insurance actually costs

Quote from the carrier before contingency removal. Premiums on Cal Fair Plan + DIC have tripled in five years. Best to know the number.

A note from your agents · § VII
JB James Beyersdorf · Associate · Land specialist

A few words on rural buying —

I came to Calaveras for a five-acre parcel in 2014 and never left. Most of my buyers since have been chasing the same thing — water, privacy, a road that ends at your gate. Some find it in Wilseyville, some in Mountain Ranch, some out past West Point. A handful have ended up across the line in Placerville. The shape of the parcel matters more than the postal address.

What I tell every rural buyer the first time we sit down: this is not the kind of transaction you rush. The questions matter more than the listing. A well report. A senior water right. A road maintenance agreement. A fire-zone designation. A working septic. None of these show up in the photos. All of them determine whether the next ten years are the life you pictured or a five-year correction.

Sara and I work these deals together. She runs marketing, negotiation, and the buyer-side of the close. I do the technical walk — the parcel pins, the well, the septic, the road. The combination is why we win these. The combination is also why we say no to some of them.

— James
