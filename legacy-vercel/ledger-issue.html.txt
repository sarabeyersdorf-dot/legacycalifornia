  <p class="lede-graf">Last Thursday I closed on a property in Arnold for a couple who first wrote to me about Copperopolis. The Thursday before that, I closed on a cabin in Dorrington for a buyer whose initial email said the words "lake access, please." And six weeks ago, I sat in a kitchen in White Pines with a family from Mountain View signing on a 1.8-acre parcel in the trees — the same family whose first call, in August, had been about a vineyard estate.</p>

  <p>Three buyers, three switches, three different reasons. But the pattern is real, and it's worth saying out loud: <strong>the highest-conviction buyers of 2026 are choosing elevation.</strong> Not always. Not universally. But often enough that I want to put it in writing.</p>

  <p>Last year I'd have called this an outlier. This year it's the trend.</p>

  <p>The Arnold column is the headline. Six buyers came in <em>asking</em> about Arnold. Thirteen ended up there. That's almost a quarter of the entire book this year deciding, after the search opened up, that the right answer was 4,000 feet of elevation, ponderosa pines, and a 90-minute drive to Bay Bridge metering.</p>

  <p>Copperopolis went the other way — fourteen inquiries, nine closings. The five who left didn't leave the region. They went up.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">§ II</span>Three reasons people are choosing elevation</h2>

  <p>I asked. I'm a real estate broker; my job is to ask. Here's the composite picture from the last twenty-or-so conversations, in roughly the order people brought them up:</p>

  <p><strong>1. Insurance, in one word.</strong> The FAIR Plan reality has reshuffled the deck. Lower-elevation grasslands carry a fire severity rating that costs an extra $4–9k a year in premiums versus comparable square footage at 3,500 ft+, where the canopy is conifer rather than chaparral. I know this sounds counterintuitive — surely the trees burn? — but the insurance tables don't agree, and they're the ones writing the check.</p>

  <p><strong>2. Heat.</strong> The lake-front buyer who came in last August spent four days touring in 108° weather. By the time we got to White Pines on day five, the car thermometer said 81°. He turned to his wife in the driveway and said the quiet part out loud: "this is the one."</p>

  <p><strong>3. The remote-work calculation has flipped.</strong> Two years ago, every Bay buyer needed to be within 90 minutes of the office for a hypothetical Tuesday return-to-site. That window is gone. The new constraint is the airport, and SAC is 70 minutes from Arnold whether you live in Copper or up the hill.</p>

  <blockquote>
    "We came up to look at a vineyard. We left thinking about a fireplace."
    <cite>— A buyer, March, on the drive home</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <h2><span class="num">§ III</span>What this means for sellers</h2>

  <p>If you own at elevation — Arnold, Dorrington, White Pines, the higher Murphys parcels — the demand curve is bending your way. Days-on-market for the >3,000 ft segment are down 22% year-over-year. Median sold-to-list ratio is back above 0.98 for the first time since 2022.</p>

  <p>If you own in the foothills proper — Copper, the Angels Camp valley, lower Sutter — the market hasn't disappeared, but the buyer pool is shallower and pickier. <strong>The remedy is not price. It's photography, staging, and a longer leash on time.</strong> The buyers are still coming; they're just doing more comparison shopping than they did last year, and they're asking harder questions about insurance and fire-mitigation.</p>

  <p>Concretely, if you're listing this spring below 2,500 feet, I'd budget:</p>

  <ul>
    <li>A defensible-space audit and a written mitigation plan, before listing. Buyers ask for this now in 70% of showings; six months ago it was 20%.</li>
    <li>A fire-zone disclosure that says the part out loud — what zone, what insurer wrote the last policy, what the premium was. Vagueness reads as evasion.</li>
    <li>Plan for 60–80 days. The 21-day flips of 2021 are over for this segment. Price right and wait.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2><span class="num">§ IV</span>What this means for buyers</h2>

  <p>If you're searching foothills properties right now and you've been looking exclusively below 2,500 feet because that's where the "wine country" pin lives on the map — please, open the radius. Drive up Highway 4 on a hot day. Pull off in Arnold for lunch at the General Store. See the temperature drop on the dashboard as you climb. You may discover, as the last three buyers did, that the home you thought you wanted was a stand-in for something else.</p>

  <p>I'm not saying don't buy in Murphys. I closed on three Murphys houses this quarter and I'd close on three more tomorrow. Murphys is its own thing and a wonderful one. I'm saying: <em>look up the hill</em>, particularly if your shortlist includes the word "summer."</p>

  <p>One more thing. The conventional wisdom says you take a discount for elevation. That hasn't been true for almost a year. Per-square-foot pricing for renovated cabins at 3,500–4,500 ft is now within 8% of comparable Murphys product, and the gap has been closing every quarter. The arbitrage is mostly closed. If you've been waiting for "the deal" up the hill, the deal is the listing — at list — before the next buyer figures it out.</p>

  <p>If you'd like to talk through any of this against your specific situation — what you're searching for, where you are in the journey, what's actually moving in the segment you care about — write me back. I read every reply.</p>
